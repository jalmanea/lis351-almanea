Fall 2021

For my first semester at UW-Madison, I took a geology course.

GEOSCI 110: Evolution and Extinction

It was GEOSCI 110: Evolution and Extinction. It was taught by Professor Peters, who is an amazing lecturer and an incredibly passionate person about this course.
So far, it is my favorite course at UW-Madison, even though it is not related to my major. The course's topics were diverse and interesting. Here are a lecture topics:

  • Finding our place in the universe

  • Finding the meaning of fossils

  • Darwin and The Beagle

  • Artificial selection

  • Variation and populations

  • Natural selection

  • Darwin's missing mechanism

  • The Central Dogma of Molecular Biology

  • CRISPR and development

  • Reproductive isolation

  • Rates of evolution and speciation

  • Telling geologic time

  • Age of the Earth

  • The Great Oxidation Event

  • The Cambrian Explosion

  • Life in the Paleozoic

  • Life in the Mesozoic

  • Mass Extinctions

  • The rock record, and macroevolution

I highly recommend this course if you find these topics interesting. It is a 4-credit (biological credit, as well) course.

Spring 2022

During the fall semester while taking GEOSCI 110, I realized that I loved the history of science part of the course.
I wanted to expand my knowledge in that field, and one course that I got several recommendations on was History of Science 202

HIST SCI 202: The Making of Modern Science

This course is taught by Professor Devin Kennedy, a lovely person to talk to and have a casual conversation with.
Professor Kennedy's lectures were incredibly organized and fun to attend.
The course covered the history of science, with Thomas Kuhn's paradigm shift view, from the mid-1500s, starting with Copernicus and Galileo, until modern day.
Here is an outline of the history that was covered:

  • 1543-1675: Revolutions in Heaven and on Earth

  • 1637-1727: Natural and Mechanical Philosophy

  • 1751-1843: Social Revolution and the Culture of Reason

  • 1735-1850: Imperialism and Order

  • 1830-1870: Evolution and the Age of Earth

  • 1850-1900: Biology and Statecraft

  • 1870-1930: Science and Modern Life

  • 1900-1938: Modernity, Relativity, and the Atom

  • 1938-1969: Atomic Sovereignty

  • 1958-1973: When Science got Big

  • 1920-1975: Biology and Politics

  • 1975-Present: Life on Earth

  • 1996-Present: Big Data, Small Particles, and Black Holes
